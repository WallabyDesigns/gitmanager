Ascent of Light: The Dark
Pursuit
by Stephen Costigan
In the Outer Marches, rule is enforced not just by soldiers, but by
ledgers, quotas, curfews, and fear. When Aric survives a Dominion
hunt and is discovered to bear the most dangerous trait in their
world, he is forced into hiding with unlikely protectors: Cornelius,
an eccentric woods-edge scholar, and Leila, his disciplined
understudy. As pursuit tightens under the shadow regime’s
deadliest trackers, Aric is driven deeper into the Untamed Belt,
where survival depends on secrecy, fractured trust, and learning
to wield a volatile light power that could either save him or expose
everyone around him. Behind the hunt, rival factions inside the
Dark Order maneuver for control, turning Aric’s existence into the
fulcrum of a larger political struggle. In a world where darkness is
organized and institutional, even hope must be smuggled, trained,
and weaponized before a new dawn can rise.

C H A P T E R  1
Where Shadows Could Not Follow
The hollows were dark where the dawn should have reached them.
He noticed it the way you notice a sound that was never there before, sideways, late,
with a cold that had nothing to do with the air. The shadows weren't following the
light. They were moving against it. Pooling between roots. Thickening along trunks.
More  than  once  a  hollow  seemed  to  shape  itself  into  something  like  a  socket.
Attention wearing no face. Watching.
Something behind the trees was directing them. Something that didn't need to run.
He ran.
The chains didn't help. The wrist lengths swung forward and caught air when he tried
to pump his arms, and the whole arrangement made noise. A constant low clink and
rattle he couldn't silence no matter how he held himself. He'd had days to live with
them. Shadow iron, the guards had called it, like that explained anything. He knew
what it explained. The first time something rose in his chest and the metal went from
cold to searing in less than a breath had been explanation enough. He hadn't done it
since. He hadn't made peace with the chains either.
Branches whipped his face. His boots slipped in leaf rot and he caught himself on a
trunk, shoved off, kept going. Every breath was a choice he had to make twice.
He  didn't  know  what  shadow-wielders  could  actually  do.  He'd  heard  stories.
Everyone had heard stories. But stories came from people who survived, which meant

===== PAGE 3 =====
they probably weren't watching from close enough. His ignorance sat in his chest like
a swallowed stone. Every patch of dark might be a hand. Every hollow might be a
mouth.
He risked a look back and wished he hadn't.
The darkness was gathering. Not pooling, not drifting, but gathering, like a deliberate
thing, pulling loose from roots and stones and the rot of fallen timber until it moved
as one. A low sound came with it, like cloth dragged slowly across bark. The forest
floor itself was answering.
He ran harder.
The ground pitched downhill into a chaos of old logging cuts choked with bramble.
He jumped one trench and landed badly, pain spiking up his ankle. He caught a
sapling, missed it, caught the next one. The wrist chain swung wide and clipped bark
with a sharp ring. To his left, a deadfall of birch blocked the straight line. To his right,
a narrow deer track threaded between thorn walls.
He took the track.
Something hissed past his ear and struck a trunk just ahead. A dart of solid black,
chain trailing back into the dark. The chain went taut, then went slack, dragged away.
He understood then. Not shooting to hit him. Shooting to steer.
They were herding him.
He cut off the track and went through hazel instead, letting branches crack behind
him as loud as he could make them. Louder than the chains, he hoped. He dropped
low, slid down a clay lip, hit a runnel of black water. Cold soaked through his boots.
He followed the runnel ten steps, then stepped out onto stone and pushed off toward

higher ground, trying to break his trail.
A fallen cedar made a low arch ahead. He went under it on his belly, scraping his
back, the wrist chains dragging through mud. He came up into a pocket of dead air
where nothing moved. For a second he thought he'd found something. A breath. A
half-second of safety.
Shadow bled under the cedar behind him. A flat black wave sliding over root and wa-
ter in one seamless sheet.
He kicked back into the runnel and the dark recoiled from the chop just enough, just
enough, for him to wrench free. He went up the bank on his hands and knees, chains
raking furrows through the clay.
The whispering thickened.
He burst into a stand of young pines, trunks close together, and zigzagged through
them  without  thinking,  just  moving,  not  letting  any  straight  line  form.  Twice  he
watched darkness surge from one trunk base toward another and cut across it. He
didn't know if it worked. He kept going.
His chest burned. His ankle screamed every time his left foot hit ground. He couldn't
hear anything over his own breathing and the low rattle of iron. He couldn't hear
where they were closing from.
He almost didn't see the gorge.
The trees gave out without warning and the ground simply stopped. Below, a long
way below, the river ran fast and pale through the mist, swollen with spring flood. It
hurled itself through the gorge as if the stone had angered it, breaking white over
black teeth of rock, folding in on itself, vanishing in troughs and leaping back up in

===== PAGE 5 =====
sheets of spray. Whole branches spun in the current, stripped clean and turning end
over end before they smashed apart.
The roar of it had been there for a while, he realized. He'd been too scared to hear it.
He stopped at the edge. His heels were on solid ground. Barely.
Behind him, the tree line still held dark. But the dark was moving, pulling together at
the wood's edge in that slow, collective way, waiting or gathering or something else he
had no word for. Three shapes stood at the treeline, men in black standing still while
the darkness pooled around their feet like water.
They weren't running. They didn't have to run.
One of them said something to the other. Calm. Conversational.
Aric looked at the river. At the rocks. At the white violence of the current. It was not
escape. It was another way to die.
Something rose in him, not courage, not quite, something older and less reasoned
that pushed up through the fear like heat through floorboards. The chains went warm
against his wrists. Then hot. He pressed it back down. He knew what happened when
he didn't.
He jumped. Fear made the choice. Desperation sent him over.
The cold hit him like a fist.
It took the breath out of him before the river even took his body. For one terrible sec-
ond he was nothing but impact and freezing dark and the iron drag of the chains
pulling his arms wrong. Then the current seized him and flung him sideways.

===== PAGE 6 =====
He did not swim. He was carried.
Water filled his ears, his nose, his mouth. The wrist lengths fouled across his chest
when he tried to claw upward, and the weight of shadow iron dragged him under at
every turn. He kicked and struck nothing. He kicked again and found stone, slick and
brutal beneath one boot, nearly losing it as the river rolled him over.
He came up coughing into mist. The gorge walls lurched past on either side, high and
dark and streaming with runoff. A wave broke against his shoulder and slapped him
under before he could take a full breath.
The river had places inside it where the water seemed to fight itself. One current
dragged him forward while another shoved him down. A boil opened beneath him
and sucked at his legs. He felt his back scrape rock, then his hip, then the chain be-
tween his wrists caught on something under the surface.
Panic tore through him.
He pulled. The iron held.
Water hammered the back of his head. His lungs clenched. His wrists twisted in the
cuffs, skin grinding against metal, and for one sickening moment he understood how
the river would keep him. Not with a grand blow. Not with a clean drowning. It would
pin him by the hands in the dark and let the flood beat the life out of him.
Heat flashed in his chest.
The chains went hot.
Not warm this time. Hot enough that pain ripped a sound from him, useless beneath
the water. Something pale flared against the murk, thin and wild, and the snag gave

===== PAGE 7 =====
way. Aric shot loose into the current, spinning, blind, burning at the wrists.
He burst back into air with a ragged gasp.
Above, far behind at the cliff edge, the three hunters had become small dark cuts
against the gray morning. They watched him go, fixed and patient, but they did not
follow. The river carried him south, beyond the reach of connected shadow, beyond
whatever they could do to him from the ground.
For now. Just for now.
Then he saw another figure.
Lower down, where the gorge bent and the bank widened into a shelf of wet stone, a
man stood alone beneath the leaning trees. He was too far for Aric to know his face,
but even through rain mist and spray there was something wrong with his stillness.
No panic. No hurry. No surprise. His dark coat moved in the wind, and the shadows
under the trees seemed deeper around him than they should have been.
Aric tried to focus on him, but the river spun him away.
A standing wave rose ahead, green-black beneath its white crest. He hit hard. The wa-
ter folded over him and drove him down, then flung him toward a cluster of rocks
with the bored strength of something ancient. He threw his bound arms up because
there was nothing else to do.
Stone clipped his forearm. Another tore across his ribs. The gorge narrowed, and the
current became a chute, fast and mean and full of broken noise. He saw sky, water,
stone, sky again. His thoughts came apart into fragments. Breathe. Kick. Don't swal-
low. Hands up. Not the rocks. Not the dark.

===== PAGE 8 =====
He came around the bend half on his back, half under the surface, and the solitary
man was gone.
Maybe he had never been there.
Maybe the river had shaken something loose in Aric's head.
A great stone rose from the water ahead, black and shining, splitting the current into
two violent streams. Aric saw it too late. The flood drove him sideways into it.
Pain flashed white across his temple.
For an instant there was no river, no hunters, no chains. Only brightness, sharp and
total.
Then nothing.

C H A P T E R  2
The Boy Without a Shadow
Morning came thin and gray over the eastern wood, with mist clinging low to the
riverbank and dew gathering along every blade of grass in tight, heavy beads.
Cornelius walked with a wicker basket over one arm and a narrow trowel in the other,
boots dark with wet soil, monocle fogged at the edge. He paused every dozen steps to
crouch, prod the ground, and deliver a quiet verdict to no one in particular.
"Too cold last week," he said. "Then too wet. Then too dry for half a day, which is
quite enough to make a fungus indecisive." He straightened. "Nature is chaos wearing
good tailoring."
Leila followed beside him with a satchel of wrapped samples and a waxed notebook
under her arm. She had learned early which of his pronouncements required a re -
sponse.
"You said this patch always fruits after first thaw."
"It does." He moved on, tapping the trowel against his palm. "When the mycelium
has manners."
They worked through birch and alder at an unhurried pace, reading the ground as
much as the trees. Leila crouched near a fallen log and brushed back wet leaf litter to
reveal a pale lace of threads in the rot beneath.

===== PAGE 10 =====
"Here. Dense spread. North side."
Cornelius brightened, all irritation gone. He knelt beside her with surprising grace
and touched the exposed network carefully.
"Good. Very good. See how it crosses the grain? Aggressive colonizer." He glanced at
her. "Write that down before I invent something less accurate."
She smiled and opened the notebook.
The morning held its quiet. Birdsong came in scattered bursts. Upstream, the river
pressed steadily against stone. No wind. No voices.
By the time the light strengthened they had three capped clusters, two shelf samples
from a birch snag, and one small tuft Cornelius declared either medicinal or trea -
sonous, pending analysis. They crossed a narrow game trail and moved into older
timber where the trunks stood farther apart and the soil ran darker, threaded with
roots thick as wrist bones.
Cornelius slowed.
"Do you hear that?"
Leila listened. A dull, irregular sound, faint and not far. Not the river. Not a wood -
pecker.
"Metal," she said.
He tilted his head. "Perhaps someone splitting kindling badly."
A few steps later they found the first sign: a boot print pressed deep into wet ground

===== PAGE 11 =====
beside a fern bed, edges sharp, heel marked with a cross-hatch pattern she didn't rec-
ognize.
She crouched. The mud hadn't filled in.
"Fresh."
"Woodcutter," Cornelius offered.
"Woodcutters don't usually come this far east."
"Then hunters." He was already looking past it. "Or someone with bad judgment and
expensive boots."
They  kept  moving.  Cornelius  talked  less  and  looked  up  more,  glancing  between
trunks  before  his  gaze  dropped  back  to  the  ground.  Twice  he  paused  at  broken
branches, clean snaps, not rot.
The second boot print appeared between two roots, then a third, and a fourth. Off to
the left lay another set entirely, parallel and spaced evenly. Two people moving in
tandem, careful and deliberate. Covering ground rather than crossing it.
Leila stopped.
She looked between the trails. The spacing was too even, the pace too controlled. This
wasn't a hunter following something. This was a search.
She held up two fingers.
Cornelius nodded without speaking. He stepped carefully to the edge of the game trail
and studied the treeline. After a moment he picked up the wicker basket and changed

===== PAGE 12 =====
direction, angling toward the river rather than deeper into the wood.
They moved in silence now.
They found him twenty feet on, in the crook where a cedar's roots had cracked and
separated from the bank. He lay on his side in the mud with his knees drawn up, one
arm folded beneath him and the other stretched out, his hand just touching the water
at the river's edge. His shirt was torn. His hair was matted flat. The left side of his
face was against the ground and his eyes were closed.
Leila pushed forward and crouched beside him. She pressed a hand to his throat.
A pulse. Slow and thready, but there.
"Alive."
She eased his face clear of the mud and checked his mouth for water or blood. His
breathing was shallow but steady. His lips were blue at the corners, his skin cold and
waxy from too long in the river.
She pulled off her outer wrap and spread it over him.
"Cornelius, clear the reeds from his legs. Carefully. His left ankle may be injured."
Cornelius stood back, not reaching for him. He was looking at something else.
"Leila."
"Whatever it is can wait." She felt along the boy's ribs, stopping when his body tight-
ened beneath her hand. "Bruised, perhaps broken. We need to get him out of the
wet."

"Leila." His voice was different now. Quieter. "Look."
She looked up.
The morning sun had finally cleared the treeline. It came through a break in the
branches at a low, clean angle, casting long, precise shadows from every root and
stone and cedar trunk around them. The mud held the shapes of leaves and twigs and
Leila's own hand, sharp as cut paper.
The boy cast none.
Where his body lay, the ground held only itself: damp soil, root lines, the faint im -
pression of his weight. The sun passed over him as if he weren't there.
She went still.
"Oh," she said.
"Yes." Cornelius stepped to the left. Then to the right. He turned his head and studied
the light from different angles, the way he read a text that refused to give up its mean-
ing, patient and systematic, unwilling to accept the first interpretation. As if he could
find the error. As if there might be one.
There wasn't. She could see that already.
"How old, do you think?" she asked.
"Young. Nineteen? Twenty?"
Neither  of  them  said  the  next  thing.  They  both  knew  what  the  absence  meant.
Everyone in the Outer Marches knew, even if most of them had never actually seen it.

Even if the stories were only stories and the registries only names on pages.
The searchers were no longer an unanswered question.
Cornelius crouched at last, bringing himself level with the boy's slack face, but his at-
tention remained fixed on the bare mud where a shadow should have been.
"He came from the river," Leila said. "See the mud on his shoulder? The current laid
it across the cloth. He went in upstream and came out here."
Cornelius examined the mark, then pressed carefully along the boy's ribs.
"Broken or badly bruised. Left side." His gaze moved lower. "And the ankle."
"The swelling hasn't set fully. Recent." Leila tightened the wrap around the boy's
shoulders. "If we keep standing here, the cold will kill him before whoever made
those tracks gets the chance."
Cornelius rose and turned slowly, reading the tree line. After a moment he walked
back to the game trail and knelt, scanning the ground. He stayed crouched longer
than she expected.
His attention withdrew behind his eyes; when he returned, he had reached a conclu-
sion.
"They're looking for him," he said. "Moving a sweep pattern from the north, angling
toward the bank. They haven't reached this point yet."
"I know."
"Taking him means taking what follows him."

Leila looked down at the mud caked across the boy's cheek, at the river water still
draining from his clothes.
"Then help me lift him before it arrives."
Cornelius did not move.
Her anger came quickly, hot enough to cut through the cold morning.
"You can debate what he is after he survives being found. Right now, he is injured,
freezing, and hunted."
Cornelius held her gaze over the rim of his monocle. The hesitation left his face,
though the calculation did not.
"No argument," he said. "Only consequences."
"Consequences can walk with us."
For the briefest moment, something like approval touched his expression.
"They usually do." He picked up the wicker basket and set it farther beneath the
cedar. "That search pattern will reach the bank soon. We leave the samples. We take
the western rise back."
"And we tell no one."
"No," he said, softer. "We cannot do that."
She had known both answers before either of them spoke. Saying them aloud made
them real, and she needed them real before she started moving.

She gathered the boy's outstretched arm gently across his chest. He made a sound,
not quite a word, not quite a name, something that came from a deep place before
language.
"I know," she said quietly to him.
Cornelius moved to take the boy's weight from the other side.
They worked quickly, Leila guiding the injured leg while Cornelius braced his shoul-
ders. She bound the swollen ankle with a folded strip from the sample cloth, firm
enough to support it without pressing the joint, then secured the outer wrap around
his wet clothes.
Above them, the light moved through the trees and found nothing unusual.
That was the part she kept coming back to. What troubled her was how ordinary the
morning looked around him. She understood danger. But the light moving over him
and finding nothing. As if he had already learned to be invisible.
She filed that away. There would be time to think about it later, or there wouldn't.
Either way, they had to move.

C H A P T E R  3
The Empty Map
Late morning light washed the high windows of the Hall of Oaths, turning the glass
pale as bone and laying hard bars across the chamber floor.
Third bell had sounded moments ago, calling clerks toward their final work before
midday. Fourth bell would mark noon. Fifth belonged to the long afternoon, and
sixth sounded at dusk, when the gates closed and curfew began.
The bells were not equal measures of time. They followed the changing daylight,
marking the Dominion's chosen boundaries between labor, travel, and confinement.
After dusk, the count began again with the night bells.
Lilith wasn't listening to the silence.
She stood at the long oath-table with both palms braced against cold stone, staring at
a patrol map someone had pinned wrong, its northeast corner curled, the Blackmile
line half-obscured. Riders had already gone east. Search teams were assembling in
the inner yard. The scribes in the side rooms were waiting on command seals she
hadn't granted yet, and the morning was bleeding away.
Along the west wall, a standing torch guttered. No wind. No reason.
Its shadow deepened.
She watched it spread across mortar and stone without comment. She'd seen it be -

===== PAGE 18 =====
fore. She knew what it meant.
The shape held for a moment, featureless and dense, then thinned at the edges and
resolved  into  a  man  in  a  black  traveling  cloak.  Lucien  stepped  out  of  the  wall's
shadow as if crossing an open doorway, no dust, no damp, no sign of distance trav-
eled. Even under full morning light, the seam he'd come through stayed darker than
the rest of the chamber for several seconds before settling back to ordinary shade.
The oath-guards at the far pillars looked down a moment too late. They'd learned.
Most shadow-wielders could cast or spread darkness across grounded lines. Some
could harden it. A rare few could assume pursuit forms. But stepping body and will
through connected shadow as if distance were cloth to fold belonged to the upper tier,
a practice confined to carefully worded instructional records under careful language
and wasn't discussed openly anywhere else.
The rank and file called it "ghost work," as they did anything they feared.
"You sent for me," Lucien said.
Lilith didn't look at him yet. She was still looking at the map. "You're late."
"I was in session. Your summons was urgent but unspecific." He waited, unhurried,
hands loose at his sides. "I'm here now."
She turned. "At the eastern river, we lost a shadow-absent prisoner from a secured
bind. Between transfer and gate report." She let it sit. "That doesn't happen."
Lucien tilted his head slightly. A fraction of movement, nothing more. "Field failures
do happen. Infrequently, but they happen."

===== PAGE 19 =====
"Not like this." She moved around the table's edge. "Someone touched that chain.
Someone inside our own command."
The room held the words the same way it held everything else, without expression,
without echo.
"And so you called me," Lucien said. Not a question.
"Who else would I call?"
"Your field officers. Your quartermasters. Anyone with actual access to the transfer
logs." He said it without irony, which made it worse. "I was not present at the eastern
river."
Lilith's jaw tightened. "I know where you were."
"Then you know this is a short conversation." He paused. "Unless there's something
you want to accuse me of directly."
"I don't accuse," she said. "I test."
He looked at her with the patience of a man who had heard better threats from more
dangerous people. "Then test efficiently. I have work."
She felt her temper spark and kept it back. Barely.
"Did you help the prisoner escape?" she said flatly.
"No."
No hesitation. No flinch. Nothing in the face that a person could catch hold of.

===== PAGE 20 =====
Lilith had questioned liars, oath-breakers, deserters. Most of them rushed to fill si-
lence. Lucien stood in it like he'd built it himself.
"Who else had access to the transfer chain?" she asked.
"That's your question to answer. You're the one investigating." He said it gently, the
way you explain something simple to someone who isn't paying attention.
Her eyes stayed on him. "Every time something goes wrong in this district, the trail
finds convenient gaps. The kind of gaps that look like bad luck but aren't."
"The Outer Marches are poorly administered," Lucien said. "Convenient gaps are a
natural result."
"Don't do that."
He raised an eyebrow. The first readable expression he'd offered her.
"Don't make it simple," she said. "You know exactly what I'm saying."
"I know what you're implying." He straightened the front of his cloak with one careful
hand. "Implication without evidence is not something the Hall recognizes. You know
that."
"The Hall recognizes whatever my father decides to recognize."
"Then bring it to him." He said it without any particular edge. "Bring Damian what
you have, and he will weigh it. He is my brother. He would hear it from you before he
heard it from anyone else, and that is worth something." A pause. "Use it."
His casual tone made the omission feel deliberate. Patient, advisory, almost kind, it

===== PAGE 21 =====
made her want to put her fist through the map.
"You're telling me how to investigate you," she said.
"I'm telling you how process works." He looked at her steadily. "You're Damian's
daughter, Lilith, not a floor clerk. If you want this taken seriously, take it seriously
yourself."
She said nothing.
"Lilith." He used her name the way he'd use a chapter heading. "If there's a breach in
the command structure, close it. I'll support any internal inquiry you want to open.
Run it properly, and I'll cooperate fully." He paused. "But I won't stand here while
you test me on instinct and waste both our time in the process."
He moved toward the side corridor without waiting to be dismissed.
She watched him go.
"The gap in the transfer log," she said to his back. "Four hours between the bind re-
port and the gate check. That isn't bad luck."
He stopped. Didn't turn.
"No," he said. "It isn't."
Then he walked on, and the corridor swallowed him.
The guards at the far pillars were looking straight ahead again.
Three years ago, the Outer Marches had the lowest defection rate under any district
command. She had audited those records herself, not as an assignment, but on her

===== PAGE 22 =====
own time, in the off-hours before anyone would notice. She knew the number. She
knew when it started to slide. She knew the precise quarter when something in the
command infrastructure began losing grip, and she knew it had nothing to do with
administration quality on the ground.
Lilith put both hands back on the cold stone table. The map was still pinned wrong.
Outside, she could hear the search teams moving in the yard, boots and harness and
low commands, the sound of organized force turning against an already thinning
trail.
Fourth bell would bring midday. By then, every team needed to be beyond the eastern
gate.
She pulled the curled corner flat and pinned it correctly.
Then she reached for the command seals.

C H A P T E R  4
What the Darkness Wanted Alive
Rain tapped steadily at the patched roof of Cornelius's shanty.
Aric came back to himself in pieces.
Bitter roots. Lamp oil. Wet wool. Pages turning somewhere nearby. Then the weight
of his body returned, and every part of it hurt. His ribs felt packed with river stones.
His ankle throbbed. One shoulder had been bound tight enough to hold the joint in
place.
He opened his eyes.
Before the sloped boards or the crammed shelves resolved into meaning, something
closer pulled his focus: a face. A young woman leaning over him with bright, level
eyes, watching with the particular attention of someone running a quiet inventory.
He didn't look away. He wasn't awake enough yet to pretend he hadn't noticed. She
was striking in the plain, unhurried way of someone who had never thought much
about it.
"Don't try to sit yet."
Her hand reached his shoulder before he could prove her right and pressed him
firmly against the cot.
"You nearly drowned today," she said. "You don't need to finish the work yourself."

===== PAGE 24 =====
Her sleeves were rolled to the elbow. Dried mud darkened the hem of her skirt.
"Where..."
"Alive," said a voice across the room. "A more useful condition than the alternative."
Cornelius stood beside the front window, holding the curtain open by a sliver. His
monocle caught the lamplight, and his considerable mustache twitched as he studied
the trees. A short blade lay on the table beside him among inkpots, parchment, and
an unlit pipe.
Aric looked down.
Bandages crossed his ribs and shoulder, stained amber with tincture work. His wrists
were bare. The skin felt wrong, numb beneath a lingering heat. Emptied. Like some-
thing that had been pressing against it for so long he had stopped knowing it was
weight.
"The chains..."
"Gone," the woman said. Her mouth tightened. "I cut what I could. Cornelius burned
the rest."
"Shadow-bind metal sings to the wrong ears if it stays near you," Cornelius said. "I
prefer silence."
Memory sharpened. The transport cart. The forest before dawn. Men who had not
hurried because they had not needed to. The gorge opening beneath him.
Cold water, then nothing.

===== PAGE 25 =====
The woman offered him a cup. "Drink."
The liquid tasted of earth and bitter leaves.
"Moss tea," Cornelius said. "Mostly legal."
She gave him a look.
"Entirely legal if no magistrate is present."
Aric swallowed. "Who are you?"
"Leila. That's Cornelius. We found you beside the river."
"And carried you here," Cornelius added.
"And you?" Leila asked.
Names were dangerous. So were strangers and locked cottages when he was too weak
to leave.
"Aric."
She accepted it without pressing. "Drink slowly."
A log shifted in the stove. Outside, wind dragged through wet branches.
Cornelius returned to the window. "They'll sweep the eastern track first. Then the
stream road. Then every dwelling with smoke."
Leila turned. "How soon?"

===== PAGE 26 =====
"Soon enough."
Aric glanced at the bolted door. "Who?"
"Dominion riders. Chain crews. Whatever loyal hounds Damian sends when some-
thing inconvenient survives."
The room cooled despite the fire.
And then the shadows in the far corner moved.
Not the way shadows move when something passes between a lamp and a wall. Not a
trick of the eye or a gust pushing at a gap in the boards. The darkness there deepened
from within, thickening as if the lamp's reach were shrinking rather than steady, and
from the center of it came a voice, quiet, unhurried, and arriving before the body it
belonged to.
"Today?" A pause that took up more space than pauses should. "All of them."
Leila was moving before the sound ended. A knife appeared in her hand, and she
placed herself between Aric and the corner. Cornelius had the blade from the table in
the same instant, held flat at his side, but the color had left his face. He did not step

The word did not make the danger smaller. It made Aric larger inside it.
By late afternoon, the fog had lifted from the lanes. Whiteridge took on color: wet
stone, gray timber, red-brown roofs, candle wax cooling in pale ranks along Maret's
workbench. Outside, someone laughed near the smithy, quick and startled, as if sur-
prised the sound had escaped.
Leila remained at the table after the others left. The copied entries lay before her.
Cornelius stood near the stair, hat in hand, waiting without quite asking permission
to remain.
At last she looked up.

"I know."

"Yes," he said. "But not as a defense. Not to buy forgiveness. When telling it helps the

Leila considered that.
"That sounded almost honest."
"It was entirely honest, which is why it sounded underdressed."
She looked down before he could see whether the corner of her mouth had moved.
"Go write your shorter letter."
Cornelius bowed once, softly, and left.
Aric found Leila later on the back step. The western sky had cleared enough for the
last light to catch on the high stone cuts beyond town. The roads there were almost
invisible unless one already knew how to look.

"Did you get enough?" he asked.
"No."
He sat beside her.
After a while, she added, "But I got something."
They watched the lane darken. Across the way, the boy at the rain barrel changed

Leila looked at Aric's hands.
"Did it pull today? The light?"
"Yes."
"And?"
"I let it be."
She nodded.
The shadow beside her boot lay ordinary on the step. She kept her gaze away from it
with such care that Aric noticed the care, though not yet its reason.
He would, eventually.
Not tonight.

Tonight, Whiteridge's lamps were being lit one by one. Maret moved through the
shop below, trimming wicks. In the grain yard, voices rose and fell as families ended
the day's work. Somewhere beyond the settlement, old roads held their silence under
moss and stone, waiting for feet that knew them.
Aric thought of the Dominion's ledgers, of neat columns built to decide where a per-
son had been and what that meant. He thought of Rupert, alive if hope was not too
foolish a thing to keep. He thought of Dahlia on the mountain, of Dudley vanishing
into rain, of the river that had carried him from one life into another.
He had wanted to go home.
He still did.
But home was no longer a single mill by a stream. It was becoming something wider,
more difficult, made of people who had chosen to remember what the Dominion
needed buried.
Leila leaned back against the doorframe.
"What are you thinking?"
Aric looked toward the high cuts.
"That roads can be hidden and still be real."
She followed his gaze.
The  last  sunlight  thinned  along  the  ridge.  For  a  moment,  every  old  route  above
Whiteridge  seemed  to  catch  it,  not  shining,  not  revealing  itself  fully,  only  giving
enough back for the eye to believe there was a way through.
